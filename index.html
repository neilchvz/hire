Origin story

How I got here

My tech journey started with fixing copiers. Seriously. I was a copier technician and solutions specialist for an MPS company and somewhere between tearing apart machines and talking to clients about their workflows, something clicked. I liked solving problems. I liked making things work better.

Originally I wanted to make things: make music with my saxophone and make cool Japanese animes as an animator. As it turns out I still do, it's just with infrastructure instead of ink.

I moved to New York deliberately, choosing it over San Francisco because of the "if I can make it here I can make it anywhere" sentiment. I wanted to be somewhere that would challenge and push me and NYC delivered. (My twin brother beating me there by a few months didn't hurt either, twin competition is strong.)

My career move to an MSP that followed was no different. MSPs are notorious "warzones" where only the strongest (or most crazy depending on how you think about it) thrive. They'll throw you into the deep end without a life jacket to sink or swim. And for someone like me, who learns "out in the field" and not in a classroom, that's exactly what I needed. MSPs don't care about your diploma, they care whether you can solve the problem in front of you. And luckily for me I could do just that, no degree required. From helpdesk to Director, all self taught.

And while my title may have changed, the hands-on work and the drive to widen my bench never did. I still design and build every day. The Director hat just means I also own the outcomes.

Direction

What I actually want to do

Here's the thing about MSPs, they're a great place to learn everything and a hard place to grow forever. You hit a ceiling, and not a technical one but a structural one. The only path forward is management and honestly? I don't want to manage people. I want to continue mastering the craft.

Advancing doesn't always mean management. You can go deep without going into people management. Senior > Staff > Principal > Distinguished.

There's something about being truly deep in a craft that I genuinely enjoy. The kind of deep where you've seen enough failure modes to know where something will break before it does. That's the version of the engineer I'm working towards.

Automation

What I'm building towards

Automation has always been an interest of mine, with a goal to automate as much as possible. But I always found it a bit inefficient and felt it still left room for errors.

Digging deeper I found a much more efficient approach, Infrastructure as Code (IaC) and the declarative model. IaC changed how I think about automation entirely. Before this, I knew automation as a reactive, step by step and command driven process. It was better than manual but still had a bit of the mundane.

With IaC, instead of telling infrastructure what to do, you tell it what it needs to be and it figures out the rest. No mundane repetition, no human in the middle passing wrong steps. I believe humans make errors and systems shouldn't depend on humans not making them. When humans are out of the critical path, errors stop being inevitable.

Automation isn't about replacing effort, it's about redirecting it toward things worth the effort.
